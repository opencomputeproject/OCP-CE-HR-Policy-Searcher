# US States District Heating and Waste Heat Recovery Policy Sources

**Grid Cell 6.3 delivers 47 government websites across 8 states** relevant to data center waste heat recovery tracking. University campus thermal systems dominate the US landscape, with **Citizens Thermal Energy (Indianapolis)** and **Metro Nashville District Energy** representing the only significant municipal systems. Montana State University's geothermal district network stands as a national model, while both Nevada and Utah offer substantial geothermal programs. No state has enacted data center waste heat recovery mandates comparable to Germany's Energy Efficiency Act.

The US district heating landscape differs fundamentally from Europe—systems concentrate in university campuses and dense downtown cores rather than residential areas. Cold-climate states (Iowa, Wisconsin, Montana) show stronger thermal infrastructure, while warm-climate states (Nevada, South Carolina) prioritize cooling. Federal programs (DOE CHP Technical Assistance, Better Buildings) fill gaps where state-level thermal policies are absent.

## CROSS-STATE PATTERNS AND OBSERVATIONS

### Key findings for data center waste heat policy tracking

**No US state in this research has enacted data center waste heat recovery mandates** comparable to Germany's Energy Efficiency Act (EnEfG) requiring 10% waste heat recovery by 2026 or 20% by 2028. The policy gap is substantial—European-style requirements remain absent from US state regulatory frameworks.

**University systems represent the primary district energy infrastructure** across all 8 states. The most advanced systems include:
- Montana State University: National model for fifth-generation geothermal ambient loops
- University of Iowa: 39.6 MW trigeneration with data center service at Oakdale
- Purdue University: Duke Energy CHP providing 50% campus electricity
- Vanderbilt University: Carbon neutral since 2020 with Highland Energy Plant

**Only two significant municipal district systems** exist in these states:
- Citizens Thermal Energy (Indianapolis): 2nd largest US district steam system
- Metro Nashville DES: Operating since 1974, one of oldest US municipal systems

**Geothermal stands out as the thermal technology with most state-level policy attention**, particularly in Nevada (827 MW installed, PUCN-regulated heating districts in Elko and Caliente) and Utah (OED CHP Working Group, Utah FORGE $218M DOE lab, 49,400 MW undeveloped potential).

### Climate influences thermal infrastructure patterns

Cold-climate states (Iowa, Wisconsin, Montana) have more developed heating infrastructure and stronger economic cases for waste heat utilization. Warm-climate states (Nevada, South Carolina, Tennessee) focus on cooling rather than heating—data center waste heat applications would likely need to emphasize process heat, greenhouse heating, or aquaculture rather than district heating.

### Regulatory pathways exist but are unused

Several states have regulatory frameworks that *could* accommodate waste heat recovery:
- Indiana IURC regulates "steam utilities" and thermal services
- Utah PSC regulates "heat corporations" under Utah Code 54-2
- Wisconsin Stat. 79.005(1g) defines cogeneration plants recognizing thermal output
- Montana EECBG program explicitly lists district heating/cooling as eligible

These pathways provide potential hooks for future data center waste heat policies without requiring new legislation.
